CNC Turning Basics: What Every Engineer Should Know

CNC turning is one of the most efficient ways to produce cylindrical parts. This guide breaks down tolerances, materials, and when turning beats milling.

← Back to Blog

What is CNC Turning?

CNC turning is a subtractive manufacturing process where a workpiece rotates at high speed while a stationary cutting tool removes material. The result is a cylindrical or conical part produced with high repeatability and tight tolerances.

Unlike milling — where the tool moves — in turning the part moves. This fundamental difference makes turning ideal for shafts, pins, bushings, and any rotationally symmetric geometry.

When to Choose Turning Over Milling

Use turning when your part is primarily cylindrical. Common examples include:

  • Shafts and axles
  • Threaded fasteners and studs
  • Hydraulic fittings and connectors
  • Bushings and spacers
  • Nozzles and valve bodies

Milling is better suited for prismatic parts with flat surfaces, pockets, or complex 3D features that can't be achieved through rotation.

Tolerances You Can Expect

Standard CNC turning tolerances run ±0.005" (±0.127 mm) without special effort. With careful setup and finishing passes, tolerances of ±0.001" (±0.025 mm) are achievable on diameter.

Surface finish of Ra 1.6 µm (63 µin) is typical off the machine. Ground finishes can reach Ra 0.4 µm (16 µin) for bearing fits and sealing surfaces.

Material Considerations

Most machinable metals run well on a lathe. Here's a quick reference:

  • 6061 Aluminum — Fast, cheap, excellent finish. Default choice for non-critical parts.
  • 303 Stainless — Free-machining grade. Better choice than 304 for turned parts.
  • 4140 Steel — Strong, tough, machines well. Good for shafts under load.
  • Brass (C360) — Exceptional machinability. Great for fittings and connectors.
  • Titanium Grade 5 — Slow feeds, sharp tools, coolant required. High cost but high performance.

Specifying Your Turned Parts

When submitting an RFQ for turned parts, include:

  1. STEP or IGES file (or a 2D print with full GD&T)
  2. Material and any required certifications (e.g., material certs, ITAR)
  3. Critical tolerances called out explicitly
  4. Surface finish requirements and any post-processing (anodize, plating, passivation)
  5. Quantity and target delivery

The more complete your package, the faster and more accurate the quotes you'll receive.
