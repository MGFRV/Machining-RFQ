Material Selection for CNC Parts:Aluminum vs Steel vs Titanium

The material you choose affects machinability, lead time, and cost dramatically. A practical breakdown for engineers sourcing precision parts.

← Back to Blog 
  <h2>How Material Choice Drives Cost</h2>
  <p>Material selection is one of the highest-leverage decisions in part design. The same geometry in aluminum versus titanium can differ by <strong>5–10× in machining cost</strong> — not just material price, but cycle time, tooling wear, and scrap rate. Choose the right material for the application, not the most impressive one on the spec sheet.</p>

  <h2>Aluminum: The Default Choice</h2>
  <p><strong>6061-T6</strong> is the workhorse of CNC machining. It cuts fast, holds tight tolerances, anodizes well, and is available everywhere. If your part doesn't have extreme strength or temperature requirements, start here.</p>
  <p><strong>7075-T6</strong> offers roughly 2× the strength of 6061 with similar machinability. Use it for aerospace structural brackets, high-load fixtures, and anything where weight-to-strength ratio is critical. It's less weldable and more expensive, but the tradeoff is often worth it.</p>

  <h2>Stainless Steel: Corrosion and Strength</h2>
  <p><strong>303 stainless</strong> is the free-machining grade — sulfur additions improve chip breaking and surface finish on turned parts. Default stainless choice for most machined components that don't require welding.</p>
  <p><strong>316 stainless</strong> adds molybdenum for superior resistance to chlorides and acids. Use it for marine, chemical processing, and medical applications where 303 would pit or corrode. Machines slower than 303 — factor this into lead time.</p>
  <p><strong>17-4 PH</strong> is precipitation-hardened stainless — high strength (comparable to titanium) with good corrosion resistance. Machines in the annealed state, then heat treated to condition H900/H1025. Common in aerospace and defense.</p>

  <h2>Steel: When You Need Strength and Budget</h2>
  <p><strong>4140 alloy steel</strong> is the cost-effective choice for high-strength structural parts — shafts, gears, tooling components. It machines well in the pre-hardened condition (28–34 HRC), and can be heat treated to 54+ HRC after machining. Requires surface coating or plating for corrosion protection.</p>

  <h2>Titanium: High Performance, High Cost</h2>
  <p><strong>Ti-6Al-4V (Grade 5)</strong> is the most commonly machined titanium alloy. Exceptional strength-to-weight ratio and corrosion resistance make it irreplaceable in aerospace, medical implants, and motorsport. However, titanium is thermally poor — heat concentrates at the cutting edge, requiring slow feeds, sharp carbide tooling, and aggressive coolant.</p>
  <p>Expect 3–5× longer cycle times versus aluminum for equivalent geometry. Budget accordingly and allow extra lead time, especially for complex parts.</p>

  <h2>Plastics: Delrin and PEEK</h2>
  <p><strong>Delrin (POM)</strong> machines cleanly, holds tolerances well, and is used for bushings, guides, and housings where metal is overkill. Inexpensive and widely available.</p>
  <p><strong>PEEK</strong> is the high-performance engineering plastic — rated to 250°C continuous service, chemically inert, and sterilizable. Used in medical devices and semiconductor equipment where metal contamination is a concern. Material cost alone is significant; machine time adds up quickly.</p>

  <h2>Decision Framework</h2>
  <ol>
    <li>Define your structural loads — minimum required yield strength?</li>
    <li>Define your environment — temperature, corrosive media, regulatory requirements (ITAR, FDA)?</li>
    <li>Define your weight target — if not constrained, steel is often cheaper than aluminum at equivalent strength.</li>
    <li>Consider post-processing — anodize, plating, heat treat — and whether the chosen alloy supports it.</li>
    <li>If multiple materials meet requirements, choose the one with the highest machinability rating to minimize shop cost.</li>
  </ol>
