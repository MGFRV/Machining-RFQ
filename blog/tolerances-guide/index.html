Understanding Tolerances in CNC Machining

Specifying tolerances too tight drives up cost. Too loose and parts don't fit. Here's how to find the right balance for your application.

← Back to Blog

Why Tolerances Matter

Tolerance is the permissible variation in a dimension. A shaft specified as 1.000" ± 0.001" may measure anywhere from 0.999" to 1.001" and still pass inspection. Every additional zero you add to a tolerance specification — going from ±0.005" to ±0.0005" — can double or triple machining cost.

The key principle: specify tolerances as loose as your design allows. Reserve tight tolerances only for surfaces that actually mate, seal, or bear load.

Standard Tolerance Tiers

Most CNC shops work within three practical tiers:

TierToleranceTypical UseRelative Cost
General±0.005" (±0.13 mm)Non-critical dimensions, clearance holes1×
Precision±0.001" (±0.025 mm)Fits, bearing seats, threaded bosses2–3×
High-Precision±0.0005" (±0.013 mm)Press fits, sealing bores, gauge surfaces4–6×

Tolerances tighter than ±0.0002" typically require grinding or lapping after machining and should be flagged explicitly in your drawing.

Title Block vs. Explicit Callouts

Most engineering drawings include a title block tolerance such as "Unless otherwise specified: ±0.005"". This covers the majority of dimensions. But for anything critical — mating diameters, press fits, thread engagement lengths — always call out the tolerance explicitly on the dimension.

Never rely on a title block for a critical feature. If it matters, put the number on the part.

GD&T: Beyond Plus/Minus

Geometric Dimensioning and Tolerancing (GD&T) communicates form, orientation, and position — things a simple ±0.005" can't express. Common callouts:

  • Flatness (⏥) — controls surface variation, not location. Use for sealing faces and mating flanges.
  • Cylindricity (⌀) — controls roundness and straightness of a bore simultaneously.
  • True Position (⊕) — controls hole location relative to a datum. More useful than coordinate tolerances for bolt patterns.
  • Runout (↗) — controls total surface variation as a part rotates. Critical for rotating shafts and bearing fits.

Over-specifying GD&T adds inspection cost. Apply it only where the geometric relationship genuinely affects function.

Surface Finish

Surface finish is often overlooked but impacts sealing, fatigue life, and mating behavior. Common specifications:

  • Ra 3.2 µm (125 µin) — Standard machined finish. Adequate for most non-critical surfaces.
  • Ra 1.6 µm (63 µin) — Typical turned or milled finish. Good for general mating surfaces.
  • Ra 0.8 µm (32 µin) — Requires a finishing pass or fine toolpath. Use for O-ring grooves and sliding fits.
  • Ra 0.4 µm (16 µin) — Ground or honed. Bearing bores, precision sealing surfaces.

Practical Checklist

  1. Identify every surface that mates, seals, or bears load — these get explicit tolerances.
  2. Everything else falls to the title block default (typically ±0.005").
  3. Use GD&T for location and form requirements that ± alone can't describe.
  4. Call out surface finish only where it matters functionally.
  5. Review your drawing against this list before sending to a shop — tight tolerances you don't need are money left on the table.
