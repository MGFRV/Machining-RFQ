## The short answer

Aerospace machining is defined less by the machine and more by the documentation, process control, material pedigree, and tolerance discipline around the part.

## What makes aerospace different

Aerospace parts often combine demanding materials, tight geometry, strict traceability, and controlled process change. The machining itself matters, but the supporting system matters just as much. If the supplier cannot hold revisions, certs, and inspection records cleanly, the spindle time is irrelevant.

That is why aerospace buyers evaluate both capability and system maturity.

## Materials and tolerances

Common aerospace work includes aluminum, titanium, Inconel-class alloys, stainless, and high-strength alloy steels. Tight positional control, thin-wall features, and controlled surface condition are common. The actual tolerance level varies by part family, but the expectation is disciplined repeatability, not 'shop typical.'

Secondary processes and approved sources can be just as important as the metal removal.

## Why AS9100 shows up so often

AS9100 builds on general quality system expectations with aerospace-specific discipline around risk, configuration, traceability, and process control. It does not guarantee a supplier can make your exact part, but it is a strong screening factor for aerospace procurement.

Treat it as a capability signal, not a substitute for technical review.

## What to ask in an RFQ

Ask about material traceability, first article process, special process control, and how revision changes are managed. If source inspection, FAIRs, or customer-approved processors are needed, state that immediately.

Aerospace parts are won and lost in the details around the print.

## Comparison table where relevant

| Aerospace requirement area | Why it matters | Buyer check |
| -- | -- | -- |
| Traceability | Links parts to material and process history | Cert package clarity |
| AS9100 or equivalent discipline | Screens system maturity | Verify scope and fit |
| FAI capability | Confirms first-run conformity | Ask early |
| Approved special processes | Controls outsourced risk | Name required sources |


## How to specify this in your RFQ

State material spec, traceability level, FAI expectation, approved special processes, and any customer flowdowns in the RFQ. Do not assume aerospace requirements are 'understood.' The good suppliers will want the specifics.

A clean RFQ does not just list requirements. It separates must-haves from preferences so the supplier can optimize where it is safe.
