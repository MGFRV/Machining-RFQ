## The short answer

Procurement teams do not need to pick spindle speed. They do need to understand that feeds and speeds drive cycle time, tool life, surface finish, and scrap risk.

## Why buyers should care

Feeds and speeds determine how aggressively material is removed. Push too hard and tool life collapses, chatter shows up, or heat builds in the part. Run too soft and the shop burns time, especially in hard alloys and deep cavities.

That is why two quotes for the same print can be far apart. One supplier may have process data, better holders, and higher-confidence cutting parameters. Another may price cautiously because it expects slow cutting and frequent tool changes.

## What changes the cutting window

Material, tool diameter, radial engagement, axial depth, coolant strategy, workholding, and spindle power all matter. Aluminum with open access runs fast. Titanium in a deep pocket does not. Small tools in tall walls do not behave like large roughers on a rigid block.

Surface finish and burr limits also influence parameters. A part that can accept visible tool marks is different from a sealing surface or a cosmetic face.

## What this means for lead time and price

If the process window is narrow, the shop may quote longer cycle times, more tool consumption, and lower lot sizes between tool checks. That is not padding. It is process reality.

A clean RFQ helps. Clear material spec, realistic finish callouts, and honest lot size give the supplier room to optimize instead of protecting itself with contingency.

## Questions worth asking

Ask where the cycle-time risk is: roughing, finishing, tool life, or inspection. Ask whether the quote assumes high-speed machining, standard carbide, or specialty tooling. Ask whether the part needs process proving because of thin walls or unstable stock.

Those questions get you closer to the real cost than arguing about shop rate.

## Comparison table where relevant

| Factor | Effect on price | Effect on risk |
| -- | -- | -- |
| Harder material | Higher cycle time and tool spend | More heat and wear |
| Long-reach tools | Slower finishing | Higher chatter risk |
| Tight finish callout | More finish passes | Higher scrap if unstable |
| Rigid workholding | May cost more up front | Usually lowers overall risk |


## How to specify this in your RFQ

Include exact material temper, finish requirement, burr expectation, and lot size. If a surface is cosmetic, say so. If finish is functional, name the area and the target. Do not leave the supplier guessing whether appearance or speed matters more.

A clean RFQ does not just list requirements. It separates must-haves from preferences so the supplier can optimize where it is safe.
