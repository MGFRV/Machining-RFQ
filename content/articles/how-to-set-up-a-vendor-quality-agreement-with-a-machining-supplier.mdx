## The short answer

A vendor quality agreement is where you turn vague expectations into operational rules. If a requirement matters repeatedly, it belongs in the agreement instead of being rediscovered on every PO.

## What belongs in the agreement

Define drawing and revision control, change-notification rules, nonconformance handling, approval for rework or use-as-is, traceability expectations, record retention, sub-tier control, and documentation delivered with shipments. Add packaging, labeling, and cert requirements if they recur.

The point is consistency, not legal ornament.

## Why it helps both sides

A clear agreement reduces RFQ friction, speeds dispute resolution, and keeps the supplier from guessing what your quality team will demand this month. It also protects the buyer from relying on memory and individual email threads.

Good agreements make day-to-day work less dramatic.

## What not to do

Do not fill the document with copied regulatory language that neither side can execute. And do not write obligations so broad that every minor process tweak triggers full customer approval. Use control where it matters.

An unworkable quality agreement is just a slower way to create conflict.

## How to implement it

Start with a practical template, tailor it by part risk and industry, and align it with the purchase order terms. Then review it jointly with the supplier before launch. A quality agreement nobody operationalized is just a PDF graveyard.

The best VQAs are short, clear, and actually used.

## Comparison table where relevant

| Agreement topic | Why it matters | Weak version |
| -- | -- | -- |
| Change notification | Protects process stability | No threshold defined |
| NCR and deviation handling | Controls bad-part decisions | Ad hoc email approvals |
| Traceability and records | Supports audits and recalls | 'Keep records as needed' |
| Sub-tier control | Prevents hidden risk transfer | No rules on outsourcing |


## How to specify this in your RFQ

Use the RFQ or sourcing kickoff to identify which recurring quality rules belong in the vendor quality agreement. Then write them clearly and keep them aligned with the PO terms. If the supplier cannot explain the agreement in plain language, rewrite it.

A clean RFQ does not just list requirements. It separates must-haves from preferences so the supplier can optimize where it is safe.
