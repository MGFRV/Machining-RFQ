## The short answer

Material certifications are not paperwork theater. They define how much traceability and evidence you are buying with the metal or plastic.

## What the common terms mean

An MTR, often called a mill test report, usually ties chemical and mechanical test results to a specific heat or lot of material from the mill. A certificate of conformance says the supplier states the material meets the called-out spec, but it does not replace traceable test data when true material pedigree matters.

Traceability means you can connect the finished part back to the material lot and often to the purchase path through the supply chain. That is routine in aerospace, defense, medical, and regulated industrial work.

## When you actually need it

If the part is structurally critical, regulated, customer-mandated, or tied to PPAP or first article packages, ask for the right certs early. Do not bolt them on after the supplier bought stock from a service center with limited traceability.

For a low-risk fixture or one-off internal bracket, full pedigree may be pointless cost. Buy traceability where risk justifies it.

## Where buyers get burned

The common failure is writing 'material certs required' without saying which level. Another is asking for full traceability on mixed-stock prototype work after the fact. Shops cannot manufacture paperwork that was never preserved.

Imported or converted material can add another layer of confusion if the source and testing path are not clear.

## A clean requirement

Good RFQs say the exact material spec, required document set, whether lot traceability to the finished part is needed, and whether copies are acceptable in the initial package. They also say whether the certs must match a named end-customer format.

That level of clarity prevents ugly surprises at receiving.

## Comparison table where relevant

| Document | What it gives you | Use it when |
| -- | -- | -- |
| Certificate of conformance | Supplier statement of compliance | Low to medium traceability need |
| MTR / mill cert | Heat or lot-linked chemistry and properties | Critical metal parts |
| Full traceability package | Chain from raw material to part | Aerospace, defense, regulated work |
| Vague 'certs required' note | Confusion | Never |


## How to specify this in your RFQ

Call out the exact material spec, cert type, and traceability level in the RFQ. Say whether the part itself must be lot-marked and whether certs ship with the first lot, every lot, or the full PPAP/FAI package. Ambiguity here is expensive and stupid.

A clean RFQ does not just list requirements. It separates must-haves from preferences so the supplier can optimize where it is safe.
