      <h2>What is Swiss Screw Machining?</h2>
      <p>Swiss screw machining (also called Swiss-type turning or Swiss automatic) is a precision turning process optimized for small-diameter, high-length-to-diameter ratio parts. Unlike conventional CNC lathes where the workpiece is held in a chuck, Swiss machines feed bar stock through a precision guide bushing. The cutting tools work immediately adjacent to the bushing, which supports the part right at the cutting zone — eliminating deflection and enabling extremely tight tolerances on long, slender parts.</p>
      <p>The result: <strong>diameters from 0.020" to 1.25"</strong>, tolerances of ±0.0002", and cycle times measured in seconds per part at production volumes.</p>

      <h2>Swiss vs. Conventional Turning</h2>
      <p>The guide bushing is the key differentiator. On a conventional lathe, a 6" long shaft with a 0.25" diameter (L/D ratio of 24:1) will deflect under cutting forces, making tight tolerances impossible without multiple passes and supports. A Swiss machine holds the same part rigid at the cut, allowing a single-pass finish cut to ±0.0002" diameter.</p>
      <p>Use Swiss turning when:</p>
      <ul>
        <li>Part diameter is under 1.25" (32 mm)</li>
        <li>Length-to-diameter ratio exceeds 3:1</li>
        <li>Tight diameter tolerances are required (±0.0005" or better)</li>
        <li>Production volume is 500+ pieces (setup costs are higher)</li>
        <li>Part requires multiple operations — OD turning, drilling, threading, milling — in a single setup</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>Common Swiss-Turned Parts</h2>
      <p>Swiss machining dominates in industries that need high volumes of small, precise components:</p>
      <ul>
        <li><strong>Medical:</strong> Bone screws, cannulas, catheter components, implant shafts</li>
        <li><strong>Aerospace / Defense:</strong> Connector pins, fasteners, valve stems, actuator shafts</li>
        <li><strong>Electronics:</strong> Connector contacts, standoffs, precision spacers</li>
        <li><strong>Hydraulics / Pneumatics:</strong> Valve bodies, fittings, orifice inserts</li>
        <li><strong>Semiconductor:</strong> Nozzles, probe pins, alignment features</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>Material Recommendations for Swiss</h2>
      <p><strong>Brass C360</strong> is the ideal Swiss material — free-machining, produces tight chips, and holds excellent tolerances. Default choice for connectors and fittings where conductivity or corrosion resistance is needed.</p>
      <p><strong>303 stainless</strong> is the best stainless choice for Swiss — the free-machining sulfur additions prevent the stringy chips that plague 304 and 316 in high-speed Swiss operations.</p>
      <p><strong>6061 aluminum</strong> runs fast and cheaply. Good for lightweight structural pins, spacers, and housings where corrosion isn't a concern.</p>
      <p>Avoid gummy, work-hardening alloys (304 SS, pure titanium, Inconel) on Swiss unless required — they cause built-up edge and reduce tool life significantly.</p>

      <h2>RFQ Checklist for Swiss Parts</h2>
      <ol>
        <li>Specify bar stock diameter range — Swiss setup is bar-size specific</li>
        <li>Call out critical diameters and their tolerances explicitly</li>
        <li>Note any live tooling features (cross-holes, flats, threads perpendicular to axis)</li>
        <li>Specify finish requirements on sealing surfaces and bearing fits</li>
        <li>Include production quantity and any annual volume estimates</li>
        <li>Note certifications required: material certs, ITAR, ISO 9001, AS9100, FDA</li>
      </ol>
