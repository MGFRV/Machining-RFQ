      <h2>Why Tolerances Matter</h2>
      <p>Tolerance is the permissible variation in a dimension. A shaft specified as <strong>1.000" ± 0.001"</strong> may measure anywhere from 0.999" to 1.001" and still pass inspection. Every additional zero you add to a tolerance specification — going from ±0.005" to ±0.0005" — can double or triple machining cost.</p>
      <p>The key principle: <strong>specify tolerances as loose as your design allows</strong>. Reserve tight tolerances only for surfaces that actually mate, seal, or bear load.</p>

      <h2>Standard Tolerance Tiers</h2>
      <p>Most CNC shops work within three practical tiers:</p>
      <table class="tol-table">
        <thead>
          <tr><th>Tier</th><th>Tolerance</th><th>Typical Use</th><th>Relative Cost</th></tr>
        </thead>
        <tbody>
          <tr><td>General</td><td>±0.005" (±0.13 mm)</td><td>Non-critical dimensions, clearance holes</td><td>1×</td></tr>
          <tr><td>Precision</td><td>±0.001" (±0.025 mm)</td><td>Fits, bearing seats, threaded bosses</td><td>2–3×</td></tr>
          <tr><td>High-Precision</td><td>±0.0005" (±0.013 mm)</td><td>Press fits, sealing bores, gauge surfaces</td><td>4–6×</td></tr>
        </tbody>
      </table>
      <p>Tolerances tighter than ±0.0002" typically require grinding or lapping after machining and should be flagged explicitly in your drawing.</p>

      <h2>Title Block vs. Explicit Callouts</h2>
      <p>Most engineering drawings include a title block tolerance such as <em>"Unless otherwise specified: ±0.005""</em>. This covers the majority of dimensions. But for anything critical — mating diameters, press fits, thread engagement lengths — always call out the tolerance explicitly on the dimension.</p>
      <p>Never rely on a title block for a critical feature. If it matters, put the number on the part.</p>

      <h2>GD&T: Beyond Plus/Minus</h2>
      <p>Geometric Dimensioning and Tolerancing (GD&T) communicates form, orientation, and position — things a simple ±0.005" can't express. Common callouts:</p>
      <ul>
        <li><strong>Flatness (⏥)</strong> — controls surface variation, not location. Use for sealing faces and mating flanges.</li>
        <li><strong>Cylindricity (⌀)</strong> — controls roundness and straightness of a bore simultaneously.</li>
        <li><strong>True Position (⊕)</strong> — controls hole location relative to a datum. More useful than coordinate tolerances for bolt patterns.</li>
        <li><strong>Runout (↗)</strong> — controls total surface variation as a part rotates. Critical for rotating shafts and bearing fits.</li>
      </ul>
      <p>Over-specifying GD&T adds inspection cost. Apply it only where the geometric relationship genuinely affects function.</p>

      <h2>Surface Finish</h2>
      <p>Surface finish is often overlooked but impacts sealing, fatigue life, and mating behavior. Common specifications:</p>
      <ul>
        <li><strong>Ra 3.2 µm (125 µin)</strong> — Standard machined finish. Adequate for most non-critical surfaces.</li>
        <li><strong>Ra 1.6 µm (63 µin)</strong> — Typical turned or milled finish. Good for general mating surfaces.</li>
        <li><strong>Ra 0.8 µm (32 µin)</strong> — Requires a finishing pass or fine toolpath. Use for O-ring grooves and sliding fits.</li>
        <li><strong>Ra 0.4 µm (16 µin)</strong> — Ground or honed. Bearing bores, precision sealing surfaces.</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>Practical Checklist</h2>
      <ol>
        <li>Identify every surface that mates, seals, or bears load — these get explicit tolerances.</li>
        <li>Everything else falls to the title block default (typically ±0.005").</li>
        <li>Use GD&T for location and form requirements that ± alone can't describe.</li>
        <li>Call out surface finish only where it matters functionally.</li>
        <li>Review your drawing against this list before sending to a shop — tight tolerances you don't need are money left on the table.</li>
      </ol>
